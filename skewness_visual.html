Understanding Skewness Direction

The skew is named for where the tail points, not where the peak is.

Left Skewed (Negative)

Skewness < 0 — tail extends left

Mean Median Mean < Median

Symmetrical

Skewness ≈ 0 — no tail bias

Mean Median Mean ≈ Median

Right Skewed (Positive)

Skewness > 0 — tail extends right

Mean Median Mean > Median

Skewness Thresholds

How go-stats-calculator classifies skewness

|s| < 0.5 = Symmetrical |s| < 1.0 = Moderate |s| ≥ 1.0 = High
Key takeaway: The name refers to the direction of the long tail, not the peak.
Right skewed (positive) = peak on the left, tail stretches right, mean is pulled right of the median.
Left skewed (negative) = peak on the right, tail stretches left, mean is pulled left of the median.
Symmetrical = balanced tails on both sides, mean and median are approximately equal.
Real-world examples:
  • Right skewed: income distribution, home prices, response times — most values cluster low with a few extreme highs
  • Left skewed: exam scores (easy test), age at retirement — most values cluster high with a few extreme lows
  • Symmetrical: human heights, measurement errors — values cluster around the center equally
